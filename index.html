The Digital Mirage

In The Digital Mirage, the CMYK series explores how digital systems break down the natural world into artificial color components. Each of the four looping hand-drawn animations C, M, Y, and K (Butterfile) represents a deconstructed layer of nature, filtered through the logic of the printing process.

The works are poetic abstractions of a digital forest, where butterflies flap in empty color channels, flowers bloom in isolation, and natural life flickers like a printer test page. The pieces feel familiar yet sterile, alive yet fragmented — echoing our modern experience of nature through screens.

Together, CMYK reflects the tension between natural beauty and digital standardization — a visual metaphor for how our world is increasingly flattened into RGBs, CMYKs, and pixels.
C

From within it, cyan digital engines emerge, dispersing cyan pixels into the air. My character sits, transfixed, while file form butterflies flutter by. This is a fragment of the void, a slice of constructed beauty rendered in a single ink.

CMYK
Size: 1688x3000 px Four second looped hand drawn animation This final artwork is unlocked only through transformation. Collectors who hold all four editions “C,” “M,” “Y,” and “K (Butterfile)” can merge them into "CMYK" through burn and redeem. In "CMYK", the digital becomes natural, just as in printing, where cyan, magenta, yellow, and black inks blend to recreate lifelike color on paper. Here, the fragmented digital illusions are unified into a complete, fabricated nature. The scene brings together the three previously isolated versions of the character, now seated side by side in a forest. The pixelated pools have become reflective water, and the digital engines have grown into tall trees with hues echoing their CMY origins. Butterflies now fly freely — not as files, but as animated expressions. It is a constructed nature, born from code, color, and illusion. "CMYK" marks the full circle of our era: where the real becomes file, the file becomes illusion, and the illusion returns as image, convincing enough to be mistaken for the world we left behind.
